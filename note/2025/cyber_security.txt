Gary McKinnon (born February 1966) is a Scottish systems administrator and hacker who was accused by a US prosecutor in 2002 of perpetrating the "biggest military computer hack of all time".[1] McKinnon said that he was looking for evidence of free energy suppression and a cover-up of UFO activity and other technologies potentially useful to the public. On 16 October 2012, after a series of legal proceedings in Britain, then Home Secretary Theresa May blocked extradition to the United States.

Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) (18 U.S.C. § 1030): The primary anti-hacking law enacted in 1986. It makes it a federal crime to intentionally access a "protected computer" (any device connected to the internet) without authorization or in excess of authorization.
Penalties: Range from fines and misdemeanors to 10–20 years in prison for causing damage to critical infrastructure, with life imprisonment possible if actions recklessly cause death.
Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA) of 1986: Prohibits unauthorized interception of electronic communications (wiretapping) and unauthorized access to stored communications, such as email.
Identity Theft Enforcement and Restitution Act (2008): Amends the CFAA to allow for prosecution of cyber-extortion, such as threatening to damage a computer or release stolen data.
Economic Espionage Act (18 U.S.C. § 1831-1839): Prohibits the theft of trade secrets, often used when hackers steal corporate intellectual property.
Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA): Criminalizes bypassing digital rights management (DRM) technologies to access copyrighted content.

United Kingdom Cyber Hacking Laws
The UK uses a mix of criminal law and data protection regulations to deter computer misuse. 
Computer Misuse Act 1990 (CMA): The cornerstone UK law for prosecuting hackers, covering unauthorized access, modifying data, or launching malware.
Section 1 (Unauthorized Access): Covers "hacking," punishable by up to two years in prison.
Section 3A (Malware/Tools): Makes it illegal to supply or obtain software for hacking (e.g., creating ransomware), with up to 10 years imprisonment.
Section 3ZA (Serious Damage): Added in 2015, this targets attacks on critical infrastructure (power grids, hospitals), carrying a potential life sentence.
Data Protection Act 2018 (DPA) & UK-GDPR: Mandates that organizations implement strict security measures to protect personal data. Failing to do so can result in fines up to £17.5 million or 4% of annual global turnover.
Telecommunications (Security) Act 2021: Imposes strict security duties on telecom providers (mobile carriers/broadband) to safeguard networks from cyberattacks, with daily fines up to 10% of turnover.
Product Security and Telecommunications Infrastructure (PSTI) Regulation: Bans universal default passwords (like '12345') on smart devices to prevent botnet hacking.
